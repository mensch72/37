How to play

37 is a game for three birds tending lichen on a single boulder of 37 cells. Each bird — Red, Yellow and Blue — guards one pair of opposite edges of the rock. You win the moment your colour of lichen forms an unbroken chain linking your two edges. The shortest such chain spans just seven cells: three birds, thirty-seven cells, a seven-cell path — hence the name.

On your turn you make a single choice: either drop a fleck of lichen from your beak onto an empty cell, or pick one of your own cells back up into your beak. Then, all at once, the whole boulder grows:

  • a cell with no living neighbours withers away in isolation;
  • a cell with one to four neighbours holds fast and survives;
  • a cell with five or six neighbours is smothered and dies of crowding;
  • an empty cell with exactly four neighbours springs to life. Its colour is the majority among its four parents, the plurality in a 2-1-1 split, or — when two colours tie 2-2 — the third, absent colour.

Fresh flecks arrive from the box and dead ones return to it; your beak only ever trades flecks with the boulder. It starts holding N flecks, and that single number is the whole difficulty dial. You may never recreate a position that has already appeared, and a bird with no lichen on the rock and an empty beak is out of the game.

Every colour is told apart by hue and by shape (Red hexagon, Yellow star, Blue circle), so the board stays clear without leaning on the red–green axis.
