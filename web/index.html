How to play

Picture a lone boulder out in the weather, its 37 stony cells slowly greened over by three strains of lichen — red, yellow and blue. Three birds tend the rock, forever picking flecks of lichen up in their beaks and dropping them again. Each bird has a favourite colour and a strange aesthetic craving: it longs to see its colour of lichen run in one unbroken ribbon from a particular side of the rock across to the opposite side. The first bird whose ribbon closes is satisfied — and wins.

37 is that story as a game. Each bird — Red, Yellow and Blue — owns one pair of opposite edges of the boulder, and you win the moment your colour of lichen forms an unbroken chain linking your two edges. The shortest such chain spans just seven cells: three birds, thirty-seven cells, a seven-cell path — hence the name.

On your turn you make a single choice: either drop a fleck of lichen from your beak onto an empty cell, or pick one of your own cells back up into your beak. Then, all at once, the whole boulder grows:

  • a cell with no living neighbours withers away in isolation;
  • a cell that is completely surrounded — all six neighbours alive — is smothered and dies of crowding;
  • any other living cell (one to five neighbours) holds fast and survives;
  • an empty cell with exactly four neighbours springs to life. Its colour is the majority among its four parents, the plurality in a 2-1-1 split, or — when two colours tie 2-2 — the third, absent colour.

This is the calm rule (S1-5), the default: only interior cells can ever be crowded, so the board changes slowly and is easy to follow. The volatile rule (S1-4) is harsher — a cell also dies at five neighbours — and roughly doubles the churn between your turns; pick it from the New game screen if you want a more turbulent game.

Fresh flecks arrive from the box and dead ones return to it; your beak only ever trades flecks with the boulder. It starts holding N flecks, and that single number is the whole difficulty dial. A beak holds at most six flecks. You may never recreate a position that has already appeared, and a bird with no lichen on the rock and an empty beak is out of the game. A bird whose whole side is walled off by other colours can no longer connect and is also declared out, its lichen left behind as terrain. And here is the twist: once two birds are out, the last one still tending the rock wins by elimination — even if its own lichen never manages to link its two sides.

Every colour is told apart by hue and by shape (Red hexagon, Yellow star, Blue circle), so the board stays clear without leaning on the red–green axis.

Opening principle & strategy

  • Claim your two sides early. Rim cells never die of crowding (an edge cell has only four neighbours, a corner three), so a fleck you place on each of your own two sides is a permanent foothold — and it sits on your shortest winning chain anyway. Securing both takes only a handful of moves and is the complete counter to being walled out.
  • The rim is a wall. Occupying all of an opponent's side permanently blocks that colour; a minimum-length winning chain can even be routed along the rim to cover one side of each opponent at once. Watch your endpoints.
  • Mind the 2-2 rule. An empty cell flanked by two of one enemy colour and two of another gives birth to your colour. A defensive wall of two colours that strictly alternates repairs itself for free against the third player; a wall built in solid blocks instead hands the attacker a free cell at the junction.
  • Keep your total at 7 or more. A shortest chain is seven cells, so a bird below seven cannot win until it breeds back up — that is the real content of an attrition attack.
